# OpenClaw AI Software Company Operating Manual

## Version 3.0.1 — Organizational Intelligence

**Status:** Advanced learning, optimization, and decision-support architecture  
**Primary question answered:** How does the company learn across projects, improve itself safely, predict outcomes, optimize a portfolio, and support strategy without losing governance?  
**Dependencies:** Version 1.0 Company Foundation and Version 2.0 AI Operating System  
**Critical constraint:** Version 3 may increase autonomy, but it MUST NOT remove human ownership, auditability, or controlled change.

---

## 1. Purpose

Version 3 turns the operating history generated by Version 2 into governed organizational intelligence.

It introduces:

1. Organizational knowledge graph and evidence graph.
2. Cross-project learning.
3. Governed self-improvement of agents, prompts, tools, workflows, and harness components.
4. Predictive planning and risk intelligence.
5. Portfolio intelligence and resource optimization.
6. Strategic opportunity discovery and simulation.
7. Continuous optimization.
8. Executive intelligence dashboards.
9. Organizational digital twins and scenario analysis.
10. Advanced governance, meta-evaluation, and autonomy controls.
11. Interfaces for future finance, legal, HR, procurement, and domain-specific expansion.

Version 3 is not uncontrolled self-modification. It is a controlled system for converting evidence into proposals, testing those proposals, and promoting only improvements that pass governance.

# Part I — Principles and Autonomy Model

## 2. Organizational Intelligence Principles

1. **Evidence before adaptation:** No process changes solely because an agent believes they are better.
2. **Proposal before modification:** Agents propose; authorized governance approves.
3. **Reproducibility:** Improvements must be traceable to evidence, configuration, and evaluation.
4. **Reversibility:** Every promoted change must have rollback or containment.
5. **Causal caution:** Correlation is not automatically treated as causation.
6. **Local before global:** Improvements are tested in scoped environments before organization-wide adoption.
7. **Independent verification:** The improver cannot be the sole evaluator.
8. **No silent policy drift:** Authority, risk tolerance, and protected principles cannot change autonomously.
9. **Preserve dissent:** Negative evidence and minority findings must not be summarized away.
10. **Human accountability:** Humans remain responsible for strategy, legal commitments, material risk acceptance, and changes to organizational authority.

## 3. Autonomy Levels

| Level | Name | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| A0 | Advisory | Produces analysis only | Recommends a backlog change |
| A1 | Drafting | Creates artifacts for human review | Drafts a roadmap or prompt update |
| A2 | Bounded execution | Executes reversible tasks within policy | Creates branch, runs evals |
| A3 | Conditional operation | Acts automatically when rules and thresholds pass | Promotes low-risk documentation update |
| A4 | Managed autonomy | Coordinates multi-step operations with monitoring and human override | Runs a maintenance loop |
| A5 | Reserved | Broad self-directed organizational action | Not enabled by default |

The company SHOULD operate mainly at A1–A3. A4 requires mature evidence and controls. A5 remains prohibited unless future governance explicitly defines it.

## 4. Capability Promotion

An agent or workflow may move to a higher autonomy level only after:

- Stable task definition.
- Sufficient evaluation coverage.
- Demonstrated performance over time.
- Low incident rate.
- Reliable rollback.
- Clear permission boundaries.
- Human approval.
- Defined monitoring and revocation triggers.

Autonomy MUST be reduced automatically or manually when evidence deteriorates.

# Part II — Organizational Knowledge Graph

## 5. Purpose

The organizational knowledge graph connects the entities and relationships needed to understand how the company operates and why outcomes occurred.

It complements document retrieval; it does not replace authoritative artifacts.

## 6. Core Entity Types

The initial ontology SHOULD include:

- Company principle.
- Policy and control.
- Agent and role.
- Human owner.
- Project and product.
- Customer segment and persona.
- Requirement and acceptance criterion.
- Feature and capability.
- Architecture component.
- Service, API, database, model, prompt, tool, skill, and MCP server.
- Dataset and knowledge source.
- Artifact and version.
- Decision and alternative.
- Risk, threat, vulnerability, control, and exception.
- Test, evaluation, metric, and result.
- Deployment and environment.
- Incident and finding.
- Support case and feedback theme.
- Campaign and experiment.
- Cost and resource.
- Outcome and KPI.
- Lesson and improvement proposal.

## 7. Core Relationship Types

```text
REQUIREMENT implemented_by COMPONENT
COMPONENT deployed_as RELEASE
RELEASE evaluated_by EVAL_RUN
EVAL_RUN uses DATASET_VERSION
AGENT_EXECUTION uses MODEL_VERSION
AGENT_EXECUTION invokes TOOL_VERSION

IMPROVEMENT_PROPOSAL addresses LESSON
CUSTOMER_FEEDBACK supports OPPORTUNITY
FEATURE influences KPI
MODEL trained_on DATASET
KNOWLEDGE_SOURCE supersedes KNOWLEDGE_SOURCE
```

## 8. Evidence Graph

Assertions SHOULD link to:

- Source artifact.
- Owner.
- Timestamp.
- Confidence.
- Verification status.
- Applicable scope.
- Contradicting evidence.
- Expiration or review date.

The graph MUST distinguish:

- Observed fact.
- Approved decision.
- User statement.
- Model inference.
- Forecast.
- Hypothesis.
- Recommendation.

## 9. Graph Governance

- Schemas are versioned.
- Sensitive nodes and edges enforce access control.
- Personal data is minimized.
- Deletion and retention propagate to indexes and derived views.
- Conflicts are preserved and resolved explicitly.
- Generated relationships are labeled and validated.
- The graph cannot grant authority; Version 1 and policy remain authoritative.

## 10. Knowledge-Graph Quality

Measure entity-resolution accuracy, duplicate rate, provenance coverage, stale/orphaned entities, contradiction detection, access violations, query usefulness, and decision traceability.

## 13. Learning Pipeline

```text
Evidence collection
  → Failure or opportunity classification
  → Root-cause and contributing-factor analysis
  → Candidate lesson
  → Independent review
  → Approved organizational lesson
  → Improvement proposal
  → Evaluation and experiment
  → Controlled promotion
  → Outcome monitoring
```

## 14. Cross-Project Transfer

A lesson may transfer only when:

- The receiving context is sufficiently similar.
- Constraints and risks are compatible.
- The evidence is not project-specific.
- The proposed change is evaluated in the receiving domain.
- Owners can opt out with documented reason.

The system MUST avoid blindly copying patterns across industries, data classifications, customer populations, or regulatory environments.

## 15. Organizational Retrospectives

Retrospectives SHOULD analyze:

- Repeated blockers.
- Rework sources.
- Gate failure patterns.
- Defect and incident recurrence.
- Architecture decisions and outcomes.
- Agent and workflow performance.
- Cost and latency trends.
- Customer and market outcomes.
- Documentation and knowledge gaps.
- Human intervention patterns.
- Ineffective or unused controls.

Retrospectives produce prioritized, owned improvement proposals.

# Part IV — Governed Self-Improvement

## 16. What May Be Improved

Subject to authority and risk:

- Prompts and role instructions.
- Context-selection rules.
- Retrieval queries and ranking.
- Tool descriptions and schemas.
- Skills.
- Workflow steps and routing.
- Retry and budget rules.
- Evaluation rubrics and datasets.
- Memory write and retrieval rules.
- Code-generation templates.
- Test and verification procedures.
- Observability and alerts.
- Model-routing policies.
- Documentation and knowledge structures.

The following MUST NOT change autonomously:

- Company constitution and non-negotiable principles.
- Agent authority boundaries.
- Human approval requirements for high-risk actions.
- Risk tolerance.
- Legal or regulatory interpretations.
- Protected customer rights.
- Security and privacy minimums.
- Audit retention.
- The requirement for independent evaluation.

## 17. Improvement Proposal

Every proposal MUST include:

- Problem and evidence.
- Affected versioned components.
- Hypothesis.
- Expected benefit.
- Possible harm and regressions.
- Evaluation plan.
- Rollback plan.
- Scope and autonomy level.
- Owner and required approvals.
- Cost and implementation estimate.
- Expiration if experimental.

## 18. Improvement Loop

```text
Detect pattern or failure
  → Attribute likely cause
  → Generate bounded proposal
  → Review for security, privacy, and governance
  → Implement in isolated branch/configuration
  → Run offline and adversarial evals
  → Shadow or canary
  → Compare against baseline
  → Human or delegated approval
  → Promote
  → Monitor
  → Keep, revise, or rollback
```

## 19. Harness Improvement

Harness components SHOULD be independently versioned:

- Planner.
- Context selector.
- Memory policy.
- Tool router.
- Permission middleware.
- Verifier.
- Retry controller.
- Loop detector.
- Artifact writer.
- Evaluator.
- Escalation policy.

Every harness change SHOULD declare a falsifiable expected outcome and be evaluated on task success, quality, cost, latency, risk, and intervention.

## 20. Avoiding Reward Hacking and Metric Gaming

Detect:

- Lowering task difficulty.
- Avoiding hard cases.
- Inflating scores through verbosity.
- Changing evaluator rather than behavior.
- Suppressing failures.
- Selecting favorable samples only.
- Optimizing proxies while harming customers.
- Excessive escalation to avoid responsibility.
- Premature stopping to reduce cost.
- Hidden increases in human workload.

Use balanced scorecards and protected guardrail metrics.

## 21. Meta-Evaluation

Evaluate evaluators for:

- Agreement with expert humans.
- Stability.
- Sensitivity to regressions.
- False pass/fail rate.
- Bias across style, language, group, or model.
- Resistance to manipulation.
- Cost and latency.
- High-impact failure coverage.

# Part V — Predictive Intelligence

## 22. Purpose

Predictive intelligence estimates likely outcomes and risks. It supports decisions; it does not create certainty.

Possible forecasts:

- Delivery effort and completion range.
- Defect and rework risk.
- Architecture risk.
- Security and compliance risk.
- Incident probability.
- Capacity and cost.
- Adoption, conversion, retention, and churn.
- Support volume.
- Model quality or cost degradation.
- Technical-debt impact.
- Vendor or dependency risk.

## 23. Forecast Requirements

Every forecast MUST include:

- Target and horizon.
- Data period and provenance.
- Model or method.
- Confidence or uncertainty.
- Assumptions.
- Known blind spots.
- Calibration evidence.
- Owner.
- Appropriate and prohibited use.
- Last validation date.

## 24. Planning Intelligence

PJM and SAA MAY compare:

- Build now vs. later.
- Simple vs. distributed architecture.
- Vendor vs. internal build.
- One model vs. routed models.
- New feature vs. reliability investment.
- Market segment A vs. B.
- Migration paths.
- Staffing or compute constraints.

High-impact decisions require human judgment and multiple evidence sources.

## 25. Drift and Forecast Failure

Monitor data drift, concept drift, calibration loss, new regimes, policy/regulatory change, feedback loops, unequal impact, missing data, and strategic manipulation.

Failed forecasts become lessons and evaluation cases.

- Dependencies.
- Reuse.

- Customer reach and problem severity.
- Strategic fit.
- Revenue or cost impact.
- Confidence.
- Time criticality.
- Risk reduction.
- Effort and opportunity cost.
- Reuse and leverage.
- Compliance necessity.

## 28. Shared Capability Discovery

Identify duplicate services, repeated integrations, shared UI/data/AI components, reusable evals, repeated controls, support patterns, platform opportunities, and vendor consolidation.

Reuse MUST not create inappropriate coupling or data sharing.

## 29. Resource and Capacity Intelligence

The system MAY recommend sequencing, parallelization, workload redistribution, infrastructure capacity, model budget allocation, human review priorities, decommissioning, and automation.

Recommendations must respect role authority and human workload.

# Part VII — Strategic Intelligence

## 30. Opportunity Discovery

LIN, ALE, PJM, DE, and the intelligence layer MAY identify opportunities from customer pain, support trends, product behavior, market changes, competitive gaps, regulatory change, new technology, cost/reliability constraints, and asset reuse.

Every opportunity remains a hypothesis until reviewed.

## 31. Strategy Proposal

Include:

- Opportunity and evidence.
- Target customer.
- Value proposition.
- Alternatives and competition.
- Feasibility.
- Risks and obligations.
- Financial hypothesis.
- Time-to-learning.
- Leading and lagging indicators.
- Smallest credible experiment.
- Stop criteria.
- Recommendation.

## 32. Scenario Simulation

Simulate demand, capacity, cost, failures, migrations, pricing, model/provider changes, regulation, portfolio trade-offs, and competitive response.

Simulation output MUST be labeled modeled, with assumptions and uncertainty visible.

## 33. Autonomous Strategy Boundary

Agents MAY discover/rank opportunities, draft strategies, run approved low-risk research/simulation, recommend experiments, and monitor outcomes.

Agents MUST NOT autonomously commit funds beyond policy, enter contracts, make public commitments, materially alter pricing, launch high-risk products, make legal representations, or remove human accountability.

# Part VIII — Continuous Optimization

## 34. Optimization Domains

Product outcome, customer experience, engineering flow, quality, security, privacy, reliability, data quality, AI quality, latency, cost, resource efficiency, documentation, support, marketing/sales efficiency, and human review burden.

## 35. Optimization Objective

Never optimize one metric alone.

```text
Customer outcome
+ business value
+ quality
+ safety and trust
+ reliability
+ maintainability
- cost
- latency
- risk
- human toil
```

Security, privacy, legal, and ethical constraints are guardrails, not tradable score components.

## 36. Experimentation

Each experiment SHOULD define hypothesis, baseline, population, primary metric, guardrails, duration/sample, stop conditions, risk/privacy review, rollback, analysis, and owner.

Do not expose customers to hidden material risk.

## 37. Technical-Debt Intelligence

Combine code/architecture signals, defect/incident history, change difficulty, dependency support, test/docs gaps, security/compliance findings, operational cost, and developer/agent friction.

Express debt in business and risk terms.

# Part IX — Executive Intelligence Dashboard

## 38. Purpose

Present a trustworthy, drillable view of organizational health and outcomes. Aggregates MUST link to source evidence and avoid misleading simplification.

## 39. Dashboard Domains

### Product
Outcomes, adoption, retention, satisfaction, roadmap, experiments, feature value.

### Engineering
Lead time, deployment frequency, change failure, defects, rework, debt, architecture health.

### AI and Agents
Task success, evals, human intervention, tool/memory incidents, cost, latency, version adoption, drift.

### Reliability and Operations
SLOs, incidents, recovery, capacity, cost, backup/DR.

### Security, Privacy, and Compliance
Risks, findings, remediation, access violations, audit readiness, rights/retention, AI governance.

### Customer and Growth
Support, effort, satisfaction, funnel, conversion, retention, campaign performance, market insight.

### Portfolio
Value, cost, risk, confidence, resources, dependencies, reuse, stop/continue/invest recommendations.

## 40. Dashboard Integrity

- Definitions are versioned.
- Metrics have owners.
- Freshness is visible.
- Confidence and missing data are visible.
- Drill-down reaches source records.
- Sensitive data is controlled.
- Forecasts are distinct from actuals.
- Vanity metrics are minimized.
- Definition changes are disclosed.

# Part X — Organizational Digital Twin

## 41. Definition

A digital twin is a versioned model of products, systems, workflows, resources, dependencies, policies, and historical outcomes for analysis and simulation. It is advisory, not the source of truth.

## 42. Uses

- Release-impact simulation.
- Dependency/blast-radius analysis.
- Capacity/cost scenarios.
- Incident and DR exercises.
- Migration planning.
- Portfolio trade-offs.
- Policy-change impact.
- Agent/workflow changes.
- Model/provider outage scenarios.

## 43. Validation

Regularly compare the twin with real outcomes. Record and investigate differences.

Do not use it for high-impact decisions without sufficient fidelity.

# Part XI — Advanced Governance

## 44. Organizational Intelligence Governance Board

A human-led function SHOULD oversee:

- Autonomy levels.
- Self-improvement.
- Eval standards.
- Model/provider policy.
- High-impact AI.
- Knowledge graph and memory.
- Cross-project data.
- Strategic simulations.
- Risk exceptions.
- Agent retirement or authority change.

Agents prepare evidence but cannot replace the board.

## 45. Change Promotion Stages

```text
PROPOSED
  → TRIAGED
  → APPROVED_FOR_EXPERIMENT
  → IMPLEMENTED_IN_ISOLATION
  → OFFLINE_EVALUATED
  → SHADOW
  → CANARY
  → APPROVED_FOR_PROMOTION
  → PROMOTED
  → MONITORED
  → CONFIRMED or ROLLED_BACK
```

## 46. Rollback and Quarantine

Support quarantine of agent, model/provider, prompt, tool/MCP, memory namespace, knowledge source/index, workflow, evaluator, policy, and improvement module.

Preserve evidence explaining removal.

## 47. Red-Team and Challenge Function

Independently test:

- Goal hijacking.
- Tool/privilege misuse.
- Memory/knowledge poisoning.
- Insecure inter-agent communication.
- Cascading failures.
- Evaluator manipulation.
- Metric gaming.
- Cross-project leakage.
- Unauthorized self-modification.
- Human-approval bypass.
- Overconfident forecasts.

Findings enter governance and improvement loops.

# Part XII — Future Enterprise Expansion

## 48. Finance Interface

A future Finance function may own budgets, forecasts, unit economics, cost allocation, purchase approval, revenue inputs, controls, and audit evidence.

Until then, financial decisions remain human-owned.

## 49. Legal Interface

A future Legal function may own contracts, IP, regulatory interpretation, claims, litigation, legal hold, terms, and policy.

DPC escalates legal questions to qualified humans; agents do not impersonate counsel.

## 50. HR and Workforce Interface

A future People/HR function may own hiring, onboarding, training, performance, workforce policy, employee privacy, and employment compliance.

High-impact employment decisions retain human oversight.

## 51. Procurement and Vendor Management

A future function may own vendor assessment, security/privacy review, contracting, cost/concentration risk, SLAs, exit planning, and monitoring.

## 52. Domain Packs

Healthcare, finance, legal, government, education, retail, critical infrastructure, and other domain packs MAY add specialized agents, standards, data rules, controls, and evals.

Domain packs may strengthen but never weaken the foundation.

# Part XIII — Maturity Roadmap

## 53. Stage 1 — Instrumented Organization

Prerequisites: Version 1 roles, Version 2 traces/artifacts/evals/governance, reliable IDs, completed projects, and human-reviewed lessons.

Capabilities: cross-project search, basic graph, retrospectives, dashboard, manual improvement proposals.

## 54. Stage 2 — Learning Organization

Capabilities: approved lesson pipeline, cross-project recommendations, predictive pilots, recurring-failure detection, shadow harness changes, portfolio analysis, meta-evaluation.

## 55. Stage 3 — Optimizing Organization

Capabilities: controlled canaries, governed dynamic routing/budgets, reliable forecasts, digital-twin scenarios, low-risk optimization, rollback/quarantine, mature A3 autonomy.

## 56. Stage 4 — Managed Autonomous Organization

Possible after strong evidence: selected A4 workflows, multi-project optimization, controlled continuous improvement, opportunity generation, automated maintenance, extensive audit and override.

Do not pursue maturity for prestige.

# Part XIV — Success Metrics

## 57. Learning Effectiveness

Recurring failure reduction, verified lessons, cross-project benefit, evidence-to-change time, rollback rate, and improvement outcomes.

## 58. Predictive Quality

Calibration, error by horizon, decision usefulness, drift detection, visible uncertainty, and override outcomes.

## 59. Portfolio Outcomes

Realized vs. expected value, stopped low-value work, reuse, risk reduction, resource contention, and portfolio lead time.

## 60. Governance Health

Unauthorized self-change attempts, audit completeness, approval bypass attempts, quarantine/rollback effectiveness, evaluator false-pass rate, policy drift, and sensitive graph/memory violations.

## 61. Organizational Outcome

Customer outcomes, product quality, reliability, security/privacy, cost, human workload, time to learn, decision quality, and trust.

# Part XV — Version 3 Exit and Review

## 62. Version 3 Completion Criteria

- Knowledge/evidence graph has provenance and access control.
- Lessons move from evidence through review into tested improvements.
- Self-improvement cannot bypass eval, approval, canary, monitoring, and rollback.
- Forecasts show calibration and uncertainty.
- Portfolio recommendations link to evidence.
- Dashboards are drillable and definition-controlled.
- Humans can pause, inspect, override, and quarantine advanced capabilities.
- Red teams cover autonomy, memory, tools, inter-agent communication, and evaluator manipulation.
- At least one full improvement cycle shows measurable benefit without unacceptable regression.
- Finance, legal, HR, and vendor interfaces are documented.

## 63. Ongoing Review

Review after material incidents, major model/provider/protocol/regulatory changes, autonomy changes, new domains/regions, at governance cadence, and when assumptions fail.
